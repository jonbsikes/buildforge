§ 00

Summary & verdict

Your DESIGN-SPEC.md is genuinely strong — it makes the right structural call (field app + office app in one shell), commits to a palette, and pins tabular figures for financials. The shipped code follows most of it. The gap between "good" and "great" is editing: the UI tries to show everything at once, loses visual hierarchy in the middle of pages, and uses too many pastel surface tints instead of typography to establish rank.

Overall
B — solid bones, under-edited surface
Biggest wins
Real mobile-first thinking (bottom tabs + FAB), tabular-nums discipline, good status color mapping, the two-track stage strip idea on project cards.
Biggest drags
Dashboard cognitive load (5 competing blocks above the fold), AP filter bar fights the table, pastel-chip badge fatigue, and the desktop nav rail is the rail from the spec but lost the 280px flyout — so mid-level nav collapsed to a single icon with no preview.
Fix first
(1) Dashboard hierarchy — one hero block, not four peers. (2) AP density + inline primary action. (3) Replace pastel pill badges with dot+label. (4) Ship the flyout the spec promised.

§ 01b

Mobile nav pattern

Good catch — you can't hover-flyout from a bottom tab bar, and you shouldn't try. Desktop and mobile share the same information architecture (Home / Projects / Financial / Manage) but need different mechanics for revealing sub-navigation. Here's the pairing I'd ship.

Principle
One IA, two affordances. On desktop, hover reveals; on mobile, tap reveals — but the reveal surface is different. Desktop flyout grows rightward from the rail; mobile "flyout" grows upward from the tab bar as a bottom sheet.

Tooltips are not a navigation surface — nobody hovers to read labels.

§ 04b

Projects index & drilldown

Looking at the shipped page today: there are two top-level sections — Home Construction (6 homes under "Prairie Sky") and Land Development (one "Prairie Sky" entry with Phase 1 at 15 lots · 8 sold and Phase 2 at 17 lots). These describe the same physical subdivision, but the UI presents them as unrelated siblings. That's the core IA bug. It's also why there's nowhere for a user to drill in — the hierarchy is flat because the two halves of the same project aren't connected.

The fix is to make Subdivision the unifying top-level node — and underneath it, show both the land-development phases (lots for sale) and the home-construction lots (homes being built), because those are two views of the same ground. Then every row at every level needs to be a live rollup of its children: no stored counters, no stale cards.

Core principle
Every row is a live summary of its children. If a lot is over-budget, its phase is flagged; if a phase is flagged, its subdivision is flagged; if any subdivision is red, the org-level header shows red. Numbers on a parent are always computed from children, never stored or cached stale.
Two project kinds, one tree
A Subdivision owns two parallel work streams that live inside it as children, not as cousins elsewhere: Land Development (horizontal work — surveying, utilities, roads, platting, erosion control; organized by Phase) and Home Construction (vertical work — individual homes on platted lots). A subdivision may have either, both, or both-at-different-phases. A land-only subdivision (you develop + sell lots, someone else builds) simply has no Home Construction branch — the lots under each Phase are for-sale products, not construction projects.
What's there today
Two sibling sections ("Home Construction" + "Land Development") that share a subdivision name but don't share a row. Inside Home Construction, a single accordion → 6 lot tiles. Inside Land Development, a card with Phase 1 / Phase 2 pills. A user can't see that the 6 homes in Home Construction are Phase 1 lots of the same Prairie Sky subdivision shown below. Every lot tile repeats the same long Guthrie address, the same "Started 12/30/2025", the same "✓ Final grade → Landscape/Irrigation - Rough" — redundant data flattens into noise.
What to ship
Five-level drilldown: Org → Subdivision → (Land Development | Home Construction) → Phase → Lot. Land Development and Home Construction are explicit child branches of a subdivision — drillable, each with their own columns and detail page. A lot carries whichever facets apply (for-sale / under-construction / both / neither-yet). Same row component at every level.

Douglas · sold + building · 62% ├─ Lot 23 · 7181 S. Same tree skeleton, different branches populated per project. A Land Development node is itself a full project with its own detail page — you click into it the same way you click into a Home Construction lot.

What every row must compute — live, from children

Active count
count(children where status in ('active','pre_construction')). On org row, sum across subdivisions. On subdivision, sum across phases. Never stored.
At-risk count
count(descendants where over_budget OR has_delayed_stage OR has_expiring_coi). This is the number that makes the row turn red and earns a left-border accent. Clicking it filters the children to just the at-risk ones.
Progress
Weighted, not averaged. sum(stage_value × child_progress) / sum(stage_value). A subdivision where one big lot is 90% done and four small ones are 10% isn't "50%" — the dollars tell the truth.
Budget delta
sum(actual_cost) - sum(budget) across descendants. Show as +$14k over or -$3k under, never just a percent. Signs matter — under and over are read opposite by a CFO.
Status dot / border
Worst of children. Red if any descendant over-budget. Orange if any delayed. Green if all clear. The parent takes on the color of its most urgent child — so a user scanning the org level sees fire locations without expanding.
Dot-row scan strip
The 6×6 dot grid under each subdivision (you already ship this) is kept, but colors map to lot state, not just sold/unsold: green (sold/complete), blue (active), orange (delayed), red (over-budget), grey (planned). Hovering a dot shows the lot name + state.
Refresh cadence
Derived on page load from a single query that joins projects → phases → lots → stages → costs. No materialized views. If a row ever shows stale data, that's a bug — not a performance trade-off. For org scale beyond ~500 lots, cache the derived row keyed by the latest updated_at across descendants so the cache auto-busts.

    Pick one. If start date matters for compliance, show it in the lot detail page; on the list, just show "153d". Every piece of text in a list view costs the user a scan cycle.

  • Stage rows duplicate information. MAJOR

    Today: "EXT ✓ Final grade → Landscape/Irrigation - Rough (4/23/2026)" and "INT ✓ HVAC - Final → Flooring Install (4/25/2026)". This is 5 facts per row (last complete, next, date) × 2 tracks = 10 facts of small text. Use the bar-of-segments from §04 — the eye catches the current and delayed ones; the full list belongs in the lot detail.

  • Four depths, one component, one data source. CRITICAL

    Stop thinking of "Projects" as a list page and start thinking of it as a tree view. Org → Subdivision → Phase → Lot. Same row component with different depth/indent. The shipped subdivision-accordion is step one of this; finish it by (a) adding a parent row with org-level rollups, (b) adding a phase-group row between subdivision and lot, and (c) merging the land-dev and home-construction trees into the same lot node.

  • Every parent row is a live rollup. Nothing is stored. CRITICAL

    "Should not be stale cards in dashboards." Every counter on a parent row — active count, at-risk count, weighted progress, budget delta, lots sold — is computed from the subset of projects/phases/lots it contains, in the same query that hydrates the page. If you want to cache for scale, key the cache by max(updated_at) across descendants so it auto-busts.

  • "Worst-of-children" propagates up. MAJOR

    If Lot 08 is over budget, the Phase 2 row is red, the Prairie Sky row is red, the org-level header shows a red at-risk count. This is how a builder sees there's a fire before expanding anything. The shipped page has no red anywhere — even though it almost certainly has at-risk lots.

  • Breadcrumb = URL. Every level is linkable. MAJOR

    /projects shows the full tree. /projects/prairie-sky scopes the component to that subdivision. /projects/prairie-sky/phase-2 scopes further. Same UI, different root — bookmark any depth, share links, browser back walks up the tree.

  • Weighted progress, never averaged. MAJOR

    A subdivision with one $600k lot at 90% and five $80k lots at 20% isn't "32%" (average) — it's ~60% by dollars. Use construction value (or stage-count as a proxy) to weight the rollup. Averaged progress on a parent row is always misleading when lot sizes differ.

  • Sort by Risk by default, not alpha or start date. MAJOR

    All 6 homes in the shipped screenshot are indistinguishable at a glance — same "Started X" / "Xd under construction" / "✓ Final grade" pattern. Sort by at-risk count desc → budget delta desc → progress asc. If nothing's at risk today, the sort collapses to stage progression and still reads useful. Expose the sort axis in the filter bar.

  • "Expand all" / "Collapse all" + memory. MAJOR

    Remember which nodes are open across navigation. localStorage["projects.open"] = [...]. A PM who expanded Phase 2 yesterday expects to land on it open today.

  • Keep the dot-scan strip, recolor it. QUICK WIN

    The 6 green dots next to "Prairie Sky · 6 homes, 6 active" is the best idea on the current page. Keep it. Map colors to lot state: green sold/complete, blue active, orange delayed, red over-budget, grey planned. Hover for lot name + status. Six green dots when one lot is actually over-budget is exactly the "stale" problem — the dots need to reflect truth, not just "active vs. not".

  • Land-dev rollup needs its own columns. MAJOR

    For a Land Development subdivision row, replace the "Active / At risk / Progress / Budget" columns with "Lots total / Sold / Remaining / $ recognized". Same component, different facet — decide per row based on whether the subdivision has homes under construction, lots for sale, or both.

  • Keyboard: arrow keys walk the tree. MINOR

    ↓/↑ move between visible rows; → expands; ← collapses or jumps to parent; Enter opens the focused row as a page. Power users will quietly love this; nobody will ask for it.

§ 07

Closing note

BuildForge already does the hardest thing well: it's opinionated. It picks a user (a small builder juggling 3–8 active jobs), picks a split (field vs. office), and commits. The remaining work isn't about adding — it's about editing. Fewer pastel chips. Ranked hierarchy. One hero per screen. Always-visible primary actions. Your spec already says all of this; the shipped UI just needs a pass with a ruler and an eraser.

"Good design is as little design as possible." — the boring but correct maxim. Every card you remove makes the next one louder.
